Buxa Tiger Reserve

Location & Area: Alipurduar District, West Bengal (~760 km² total; core ~385, buffer ~375 km²). Established as Tiger Reserve in 1983, national park notified in 1997

Geography & Habitat

Situated at 125 m to 1,750 m elevation in the eastern Himalaya foothills. Forest types include sal savannah, moist deciduous, evergreen, sub-Himalayan wet hill, Bhabar & Terai forests.

Flora & Fauna

  • Flora: Home to 450+ tree species, 250 shrubs, 400 herbs, 10 bamboo, 150 orchids, 100 grasses, 130 aquatic flora with 70 sedges, and diverse ferns & climbers. Dominant trees include sal, champa, gamhar, simul, chikrasi.
  • Mammals: 73 species including Bengal tiger, Indian leopard, clouded leopard, gaur, elephants, wild boar, chital, hispid hare, Chinese pangolin, and Asiatic golden cat (recently recorded).
  • Birds & Others: Over 284 species of birds including great hornbill, rufous-necked hornbill, black stork, ibisbill, migratory goosanders, wagtails, teal and black-necked crane. Also rich in reptiles, amphibians, fishes, butterflies (~400 species).

Activities & Attractions

  • Jeep safaris from Rajabhatkhawa and Jayanti ranges through forest core and riverbeds for wildlife viewing.
  • Trekking to Buxa Fort, Lepchakha, Rover’s Point (1,400 m), and Mahakal Cave along Jayanti River and hills of Bhutan border.
  • Butterfly watching (400 species), birdwatching, visits to historic Buxa Fort (former prison) and Rajabhatkhawa Nature Interpretation Centre.

Accessibility & Best Season

Accessible via Alipurduar (10 km) and Siliguri. Nearest rail: Alipurduar Junction; airport: Bagdogra (~180–195 km). Open October–April; closed mid‑June to mid‑September during monsoon.

Conservation & Recent Highlights

Faced with challenges like historic dolomite mining, habitat degradation, and poaching, Buxa now emphasizes habitat restoration, anti-poaching, and community-driven conservation. A significant hornbill survey (2019–2021) estimated ~8,050 oriental pied hornbills (~11/km²) nesting successfully (~81 %) :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}. In 2022, the clearwing moth Scoliokona Kallies was recorded for the first time in India here.
